About

The dependency ratio is one of the most-cited single numbers in demography because it sits on top of two slow-moving inputs — fertility roughly 20 years ago and life expectancy — and both are observable today. That makes the next few decades of a country's demographic trajectory unusually predictable.

A high or rising dependency ratio means a smaller pool of working-age adults is supporting a larger pool of dependents (children plus retirees). Whether the rise is driven by youth dependency (high birth rates) or old-age dependency (population aging) tells very different stories about the economy, fiscal policy, and the labour market.

This page lets you explore that quantitatively: pick any country, region, or the world, compare them, then run "what if" scenarios by overriding three demographic levers — total fertility rate, life expectancy at birth, and net migration. The model framework is inspired by Max Fisher's How China blew up its own future.

Data sources

All real-data baselines come from the United Nations World Population Prospects 2024 (UN Department of Economic and Social Affairs, Population Division), distributed in tidy form by Our World in Data. Specific OWID grapher slugs consumed by the build pipeline:

  • age-dependency-ratio-projected-to-2100 — total dependency ratio, historical + medium-variant projection.
  • age-dependency-ratio-old — old-age dependency ratio.
  • age-dependency-ratio-young-of-working-age-population — youth dependency ratio.
  • fertility-rate-with-projections — total fertility rate, historical + projection.
  • life-expectancy — life expectancy at birth.
  • population-young-working-elderly-with-projections — population by broad age group.
  • population-by-five-year-age-group — 5-year age group structure (the projection seed).
  • population-unwpp — total population.
  • population-growth-rate-with-and-without-migration — derives net migration as growth − natural change.

The raw CSVs are kept in the repository under raw-data/ and the build script that turns them into bundled JSON lives at scripts/build-data.py, so anyone can rebuild the data pipeline end-to-end.

Formula & how to read the value

How to read the value

  1. ≤ 50%
    Healthy

    Enough working-age adults to support the dependent population. Pension and education systems run sustainably.

  2. 50–65%
    Strain

    Fewer workers available per dependent. Pressure builds on social spending, healthcare, and the labour market.

  3. 65–80%
    Decline

    The economy is structurally aging. Tax base shrinks; productivity per worker has to keep rising just to hold the line.

  4. > 80%
    Crisis

    The working-age base may no longer fund pensions, healthcare, and child support without policy intervention or imported labour.

Dependency ratio ↔ Support ratio

The support ratio is the inverse of the dependency ratio.

Support ratio = 100 dependency ratio
Dependency ratio Support ratio Plain-language reading
50%2.002 workers per dependent
65%1.54~1.5 workers per dependent
80%1.251.25 workers per dependent
100%1.001:1 — one worker per dependent
120%0.83fewer than one worker per dependent

The financial intuition: every retiree's pension and every child's schooling is funded — directly or indirectly — by working-age contributions. A support ratio of 2.0 means each worker is funding half a dependent; at 1.0 they're funding a whole one; below 1.0 each worker is on the hook for more than one. That is what makes the slope of the dependency ratio matter so much for fiscal policy, retirement systems, and labour markets.

Replacement rate: 2.1

The replacement rate is the average number of children per woman needed to keep a population stable across generations. The fundamental question: do daughters outnumber, equal, or fall short of mothers?

The math starts at 2.0 — one daughter and, on average, one son per woman. Two adjustments push it higher:

  • Sex ratio at birth. About 105 boys are born per 100 girls, so producing one daughter requires ~2.05 births.
  • Child mortality. Some girls don't survive to reproductive age, adding another ~0.05.

Result: ~2.1 children per woman in low-mortality countries. Historically, when child mortality was high, replacement required 4+ births per woman.

Note: life expectancy doesn't enter the formula. Only survival through the childbearing years matters — a woman who lives to 45 contributes the same as one who lives to 95.

Methodology

Real-data baseline

Historical estimates 1950–2023 and projected medium-variant values 2024–2100 come from the UN World Population Prospects 2024, distributed via Our World in Data. We use the dependency ratio, total fertility rate, life expectancy at birth, and population by broad and 5-year age groups directly. Net migration rate is derived as (population growth rate) − (natural change rate).

Custom-scenario projection engine

The scenario engine is a deterministic single-sex cohort-component model in 5-year age groups and 5-year time steps. Starting from the 2023 5-year age structure, each step:

  1. Ages each cohort forward one bin, applying age-specific survival ratios interpolated to the chosen life expectancy.
  2. Generates births = Σ (women in 5-year reproductive group × total fertility rate × ASFR-share), with women assumed to be 49% of each cohort and shares from the chosen ASFR pattern (early/mid/late peak).
  3. Survives newborns to ages 0–4 using a birth-survival ratio also indexed by life expectancy.
  4. Adds net migrants = (total population × net-migration rate / 1000 × 5), distributed across age groups using a typical migrant age profile (peaks at 20–34).

Survival ratios and birth-survival are stored at six reference life expectancies (50, 60, 70, 80, 85, 90) and linearly interpolated. Reference values are calibrated to a UN-style abridged general-pattern model life table. The model is intentionally simple — it is for "what would change if" scenario thinking, not for replacing UN demographic forecasts.

Limitations

  • No sex disaggregation: combined-sex with a fixed women-share (49%). Real fertility & mortality differ by sex.
  • Constant scenario inputs across the projection window — total fertility rate, life expectancy, and migration rate are held fixed; UN's variants modulate these over time.
  • Migration is exogenous and uniform per-year; surges and selectivity by skill/age beyond the standard pattern are not modeled.
  • The standard ASFR pattern is one of three preset shapes; real countries have unique patterns.
